Silent, Silent night

"Silent, Silent Night" by William Blake is a dark and pessimistic poem that explores the dangers of joy. The poem's four stanzas each offer a different perspective on joy, from its initial allure to its ultimate destruction. The first stanza presents joy as a tempting but dangerous force, a "holy light" that can easily be "quenched." The second stanza reveals that joy is often accompanied by spirits that "betray" us, leading us into deceit and sorrow. The third stanza asks why joy should be sweet if it is ultimately harmful, and the fourth stanza concludes that true joy is impossible because it is always corrupted by deceit.

Silent, silent night,

Quench the holy light

Of thy torches bright;

For possessed of Day

Thousand spirits stray

That sweet joys betray.

Why should joys be sweet

Used with deceit,

Nor with sorrows meet?

But an honest joy

Does itself destroy

For a harlot coy.
