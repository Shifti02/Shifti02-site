Analysis

To Tirzah

"To Tirzah" explores the speaker's ambivalent relationship with his female creator, Tirzah. The poem contrasts the limitations of mortal birth and the freedom of spiritual rebirth. Unlike "The Tyger" and "The Lamb," this poem is a more personal meditation. It reflects the era's preoccupation with the fall of man and the need for salvation. The speaker's condemnation of Tirzah and his reliance on Jesus align with the religious revivalism of the time.

Whate'er is Born of Mortal Birth

Must be consumed with the Earth

To rise from Generation free:

Then what have I to do with thee?

The Sexes sprung from Shame & Pride,

Blow'd in the morn, in evening died;

But Mercy chang'd Death into Sleep;

The Sexes rose to work & weep.

Thou, Mother of my Mortal part,

With cruelty didst mould my Heart,

And with false self-deceiving tears

Didst bind my Nostrils, Eyes, & Ears:

Didst close my Tongue in senseless clay,

And me to Mortal Life betray.

The Death of Jesus set me free:

Then what have I to do with thee?
