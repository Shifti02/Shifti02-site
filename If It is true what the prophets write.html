Analysis

If It is true what the prophets write

This poem, by an unknown author, challenges religious orthodoxy and the authority of the established church. It questions the validity of pagan gods and the worship of idols, arguing that they are mere "stocks and stones" and that true worship belongs to the one true God. The poem reflects the anti-establishment sentiment common in the Romantic period and aligns with William Blake's own disdain for organized religion, which he perceived as oppressive and corrupt.

If it is true, what the Prophets write,

That the heathen gods are all stocks and stones,

Shall we, for the sake of being polite,
