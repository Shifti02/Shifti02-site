Analysis

The Book Of Urizen: the preludium

This poem introduces the figure of Urizen, a central character in Blake's mythology. Urizen represents reason and law, and his exclusion from the realm of the Eternals highlights the tension between these and his own principles. The poem's language is stark and direct, contrasting with the more lyrical and symbolic style of some of Blake's other works. It reflects the poem's focus on Urizen's isolation and the dark visions he brings. Compared to Blake's other works, this poem is more concise and less allegorical. It lacks the rich imagery and complex symbolism of works like "The Tyger" or "Auguries of Innocence."

Of the primeval Priests assum'd power,

When Eternals spurn'd back his religion;

And gave him a place in the north,

Obscure, shadowy, void, solitary.

Eternals I hear your call gladly,

Dictate swift winged words, & fear not

To unfold your dark visions of torment.
