Analysis

The Fairy

The poem captures the sense of wonder and awe that children often experience when encountering the natural world. The encounter with the fairy represents a moment of enchantment and magic, where the boundaries between reality and imagination blur. Like much of Blake's work, "The Fairy" contains symbolic and allegorical elements. The fairy can be interpreted as a symbol of imagination, creativity, or spiritual insight. The poem may also be seen as a metaphor for the power of art and poetry to transport us to realms of enchantment and wonder, where we can experience moments of transcendent beauty and truth.

‘COME hither, my Sparrows,

My little arrows.

If a tear or a smile

Will a man beguile,

If an amorous delay

Clouds a sunshiny day,

If the step of a foot

Smites the heart to its root,

'Tis the marriage-ring…

Makes each fairy a king.'

So a Fairy sung.

From the leaves I sprung;

He leap'd from the spray

To flee away;

But in my hat caught,

He soon shall be taught.

Let him laugh, let him cry,

He's my Butterfly;

For I've pull'd out the sting

Of the marriage-ring.
